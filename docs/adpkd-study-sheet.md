# ADPKD Study Sheet

Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease. Synthesized from UpToDate (epidemiology/clinical/diagnosis, treatment, extrarenal manifestations topics).

---

## 1. Genetics & Epidemiology

- Prevalence: ~1 in 1000 live births; most common monogenic cause of CKD/ESKD.
- **PKD1** (chromosome 16): ~78% of families. More severe phenotype, earlier ESKD.
- **PKD2** (chromosome 4): ~14% of families. Milder course, later ESKD, but still not benign.
- Small minority: other genes (e.g., GANAB, DNAJB11, IFT140, ALG9) — "rare genotypes."
- Virtually 100% penetrance by imaging in PKD1/PKD2 carriers who live long enough.

**Risk factors for faster progression:** PKD1 mutation (esp. truncating), male sex, early-onset symptoms, large total kidney volume (TKV), hypertension, early hematuria.

---

## 2. Clinical Presentation

Most patients are asymptomatic until adulthood. Think ADPKD when you see:

- **Hypertension** — often the earliest finding, years before GFR drops.
- **Flank/abdominal pain** — from cyst hemorrhage, stones, or infection. Most common symptom.
- **Hematuria** — gross or microscopic, often after cyst rupture.
- **Proteinuria** — usually sub-nephrotic; heavy proteinuria should prompt another diagnosis.
- **Nephrolithiasis** — ~20%; often uric acid or calcium oxalate.
- **UTI / cyst infection** — fever + localized flank pain; blood cultures often positive.
- **Palpable kidneys / abdominal mass** — with advanced disease.
- **Incidental finding** — large cystic kidneys on imaging done for other reasons.

---

## 3. Diagnosis

### When to suspect
Family history + clinical features, OR incidental cystic kidneys on imaging.

### Imaging is the primary tool
Ultrasound first if patient is asymptomatic with normal GFR and a known family history. Go to CT or MRI if:
- US is equivocal,
- Patient has palpable kidneys or reduced eGFR,
- Incidental masses/complex cysts need characterization,
- Evaluating a potential kidney donor.

### Ultrasound criteria (family history known — Pei/unified criteria, by age)
| Age | Cyst count to diagnose |
|---|---|
| 15–39 y | ≥3 (uni- or bilateral) |
| 40–59 y | ≥2 in **each** kidney |
| ≥60 y | ≥4 in **each** kidney |

### No family history
Diagnose if **≥10 cysts (≥5 mm) in each kidney**, especially with enlarged kidneys or liver cysts, and no features of an alternate cystic disorder. Offer **genetic testing** in these patients.

### When to use genetic testing (next-gen PKD panel)
- Equivocal imaging (e.g., evaluating a living donor).
- Atypical presentation.
- Sporadic disease, no family history.
- Syndromic features.
- Reproductive counseling / pre-implantation diagnosis.
- Known rare-genotype family.

### Differential diagnosis of multiple kidney cysts
- Simple cysts (age-related)
- ARPKD (infants/children, hepatic fibrosis)
- Autosomal dominant tubulointerstitial kidney disease
- Tuberous sclerosis complex, Von Hippel–Lindau
- Acquired cystic disease of dialysis
- Medullary sponge kidney, medullary cystic kidney disease
- Orofaciodigital syndrome type 1

## 4. Risk Stratification (who gets tolvaptan?)

### Mayo Clinic Imaging Classification (preferred)
- Based on **age, height, and total kidney volume (TKV)** from CT (no contrast) or MRI (no gadolinium).
- Class 1A–1E: typical bilateral disease, stratified by height-adjusted TKV.
- **Classes 1C, 1D, 1E = high risk** for progression to ESKD → tolvaptan candidates.
- Class 2 = atypical imaging (asymmetric, unilateral, segmental) — usually slower progression.

### Alternative predictors if TKV not available
- PROPKD score (genotype, sex, early HTN, early urologic events)
- Rapid eGFR decline (≥2.5–3 mL/min/1.73 m²/yr)
- Early-onset HTN (<35 y), early-onset gross hematuria

## 5. Treatment

### General measures — all patients
- **BP control** (cornerstone):
  - Age 18–50 with eGFR >60: target **≤110/75** with ACEi or ARB (home or ambulatory BP).
  - Others: standard CKD BP targets with ACEi/ARB first.
- **Sodium** restriction: <2 g/day.
- **High fluid intake**: >3 L/day if eGFR ≥30 and no hyponatremia risk — suppresses vasopressin/cAMP.
- **Avoid** SGLT2 inhibitors in ADPKD *unless* needed for heart failure (may raise vasopressin; ADPKD patients excluded from pivotal trials).
- Statins per usual CKD indications; smoking cessation; avoid nephrotoxins and prolonged NSAIDs.

### Tolvaptan (V2 receptor antagonist) — the only disease-modifying drug
- **Who**: high-risk adults with eGFR **≥25 mL/min/1.73 m²** (Mayo 1C/1D/1E or equivalent).
- **Effect**: slows rate of eGFR decline and TKV growth; does not cure.
- **Key side effects**: aquaresis → polyuria, nocturia, thirst; **hepatotoxicity** (idiosyncratic, can be severe).
- **Contraindicated** with liver impairment (except uncomplicated polycystic liver disease).
- **REMS program** required — prescriber must enroll; monthly LFTs for first 18 months, then q3 months.
- Counsel: expensive; adherence limited by polyuria.

### Managing specific complications
- **Cyst pain**: stepwise — acetaminophen, tramadol, avoid chronic NSAIDs; cyst decompression (aspiration/sclerosis, laparoscopic fenestration) in refractory cases.
- **Hematuria**: usually self-limited; bed rest, hydration; rule out stone, infection, malignancy.
- **Stones**: standard management; uric acid stones common → alkalinize urine.
- **Cyst infection**: lipophilic antibiotics (fluoroquinolone, TMP-SMX) for weeks; imaging to guide duration.
- **ESKD**: all modalities work. Peritoneal dialysis is feasible despite large kidneys. Transplant outcomes equal to or better than non-ADPKD.
- **Nephrectomy**: reserved for disabling mass symptoms, abdominal wall hernias from mass, suspected RCC, recurrent infection, or to make room for a transplant.

## 6. Extrarenal Manifestations

### Intracranial aneurysms — the most feared
- Prevalence in ADPKD: **~8–12%** (vs ~2% general population).
- Ruptures: larger aneurysms, often <50 y old, poorly controlled HTN, family history.
- **Screening indications** (CTA or time-of-flight MRA — no gadolinium needed):
  - Prior SAH
  - Family history of aneurysm, SAH, or unexplained sudden death (esp. 1st-degree relative)
  - Upcoming major elective surgery
  - High-risk occupations (pilots, etc.)
  - Chronic anticoagulation
- **Rescreening**: every 5 years if family history of intracranial bleeding/CVA.
- Small unruptured aneurysms → surveillance imaging per general neurovascular guidelines.

### Hepatic cysts
- Most common extrarenal feature; prevalence rises with age, higher in women (estrogen-driven).
- Usually asymptomatic, preserved LFTs.
- Symptomatic massive polycystic liver disease: pain, early satiety → aspiration/sclerosis, fenestration, hepatic resection, or transplant in severe cases.
- Cyst infection: fever + RUQ pain; lipophilic antibiotics.

### Cardiac
- **Valvular**: mitral valve prolapse (~25%), aortic regurgitation.
- Coronary artery aneurysms (rare).
- Asymptomatic pericardial effusions.
- Echo only if murmur or symptoms — not routine.

### Other
- **Colonic diverticula** (more common, more likely to perforate — esp. post-transplant).
- **Abdominal wall / inguinal hernias**.
- **Seminal vesicle cysts** (rarely cause infertility).
- **Pancreatic cysts** — typically benign, no routine screening.
- **Bronchiectasis**, arachnoid cysts — less common.

## 7. Quick Pearls for Rotations

- Normotensive 25-year-old with a PKD parent and new HTN → think ADPKD even before imaging.
- Don't overinterpret a few simple cysts in a 55-year-old without family history; age-related cysts are common.
- Heavy proteinuria is NOT typical for ADPKD — go looking for something else.
- Hyponatremia on tolvaptan usually means under-drinking; titrate up only with adequate free-water intake.
- New sudden severe headache in an ADPKD patient = SAH until proven otherwise.
- Hepatic cyst symptoms track with estrogen exposure; revisit OCPs/HRT.
- Living donor evaluation in an at-risk family member: MRI + genetic testing if imaging is equivocal.
